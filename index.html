Selling the Message

Discourse markers, filler language, and persuasive writing — through the lens of advertising.

Objectives

  • Identify and use discourse markers to organize and connect ideas clearly in speech and writing.
  • Recognize and appropriately use filler sounds and words to sound more natural and fluent in spoken English.
  • Analyze how persuasive and evaluative language is used to discuss advertising practices.
  • Plan and write a well-organized for-and-against paragraph on a real advertising issue, using discourse markers to link arguments.

What are discourse markers?

Discourse markers are words and phrases that organize what we say or write. They show the logical relationship between ideas — adding, contrasting, giving examples, sequencing, or concluding. Advertisements use them constantly to build a persuasive argument.

➕ Adding ideas

in additionmoreoverfurthermorealso
AD COPYThis cream reduces wrinkles. In addition, it protects your skin from sun damage.

⚖️ Contrasting

howeveron the other handalthougheven so
AD COPYThe photo looks flawless. However, it was heavily edited before publication.

💡 Giving examples

for examplefor instancesuch aslike
AD COPYSome brands, such as beauty and fashion companies, edit almost every image they publish.

🔢 Sequencing

firstlythenafter thatfinally
AD COPYFirstly, the company chooses a model. Then, they take the photos. Finally, they edit the image digitally.

🎯 Concluding / result

as a resultin conclusionoveralltherefore
AD COPYAs a result, many young people feel pressure to look like the models in these ads.

What are filler sounds and words?

Fillers don't add logical meaning like discourse markers do. Instead, they help a speaker buy thinking time, sound more natural, or soften what they're about to say. Native speakers use them constantly — including in ad-agency pitch meetings!

🤔 Thinking time

umuhwelllet me think
PITCH MEETING"So, um, we were thinking the campaign could focus on... uh, sustainability?"

🗣️ Keeping the floor / connecting

you knowI meanlikeso
PITCH MEETING"It's, you know, a bold idea, but I mean, it could really work."

✅ Softening / reacting

actuallyrightbasicallykind of
PITCH MEETING"Actually, the client wants something kind of edgier."
GOOD TO KNOWFillers are natural and useful in speaking — but they should almost never appear in formal writing, like the for-and-against paragraph you'll write in this lesson.

A · Read and analyze

Read the essay prompt and one student's answer. Then decide whether the student has really answered the question.

ESSAY PROMPT

"Photoshopped images should be banned in ads." Discuss the arguments for and against this statement.

STUDENT'S ANSWER In recent years, some advertising companies have been criticized for using photoshopped images in their advertising campaigns. For example, it's common for the pictures in ads for some products — beauty products, for instance — to be edited to make the people in the ads appear perfect. The ad companies use computer technology to change the shape of people's bodies. Due to this trend, young people can have health problems because they try to look like the people in the ads. As a result, photoshopping ads has already been banned in some countries. In France, for example, photoshopped images are not shown in magazines or on billboards. I agree that it should now be banned in the rest of the world, too.

Discussion question

Has the student answered the question? (The prompt asks for arguments for and against.)

Answer: No — the student only gives arguments for the ban. There are no arguments against it (for example: banning photoshop could restrict artistic/commercial freedom, be hard to enforce, or reduce advertising quality). A for-and-against paragraph must present both sides before giving an opinion.

B · Learn: how to write for-and-against arguments

A for-and-against paragraph needs a clear, balanced structure. Study the pattern below before you write your own.

🧱 The structure

1. Introduce the topic neutrally
2. Arguments for
3. Arguments against
4. Your opinion / conclusion

🗣️ Introducing arguments FOR

Those in favor argue that...Supporters claim that...One argument for... is that...

🗣️ Introducing arguments AGAINST

On the other hand...Opponents believe that...However, some people feel that...

➕ Adding a second point

In addition...A further point is that...What's more...

🎯 Giving your opinion

Overall, I believe that...In conclusion...Taking everything into account...
MODEL EXAMPLE Nowadays, many advertising companies edit their photos before publishing them. Those in favor of this practice argue that advertisements are meant to look attractive, not realistic — after all, they exist to sell a product. In addition, some argue that photo editing is simply a form of digital art. On the other hand, opponents believe that photoshopped ads create unrealistic beauty standards. What's more, this can seriously affect the self-esteem of young people. Overall, I believe advertisers should be more transparent about the images they use.

C · Your turn: write a for-and-against paragraph

Task: Write a for-and-against paragraph about photoshopping in advertisements. Unlike the student sample, make sure you present both sides before giving your own opinion.

  1. Analyze the prompt

    Underline the key task words. This prompt asks you to discuss arguments for and against — not just to argue one side.

  2. Brainstorm both sides

    List at least 2 arguments for banning photoshopped ads and 2 arguments against banning them.

  3. Organize your ideas

    Plan your paragraph: introduction → arguments for → arguments against → your conclusion/opinion.

  4. Draft your paragraph

    Write your first version. Use discourse markers from Part 1 (however, in addition, for example, as a result...) to connect your ideas clearly.

  5. Revise and check

    Reread your paragraph. Remove any filler words (um, like, you know) — these belong in speech, not formal writing. Check that both sides are represented.

Sentence starters you can use

  • Introduction: "Nowadays, it is common for advertising companies to..."
  • Arguments for the ban: "Those in favor of banning photoshopped ads argue that... For example,... In addition,..."
  • Arguments against the ban: "On the other hand, some people believe that... For instance,..."
  • Conclusion/opinion: "Overall, I believe that... / In conclusion,..."
